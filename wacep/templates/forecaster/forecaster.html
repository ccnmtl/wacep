Overview

About the Activity

In this four-phase exercise, we aim to give you a sense for the different steps involved in the generation of a basic climate forecast. We analyze thirty years of data that reflects ENSO and hurricane/named storm activity throughout the Atlantic Basin in the Caribbean. Data is sourced from 1966-2012 Atlantic Storm/Hurricanes from NOAA HURDAT, 2013 storm/hurricanes from Unisys, and 1966-2013 Aug-Sep-Oct NINO3.4 SST anomalies from the International Research Institute for Climate and Society.

The first phase, "Initial Analysis," allows you to observe ENSO trends over time, hurricane occurrence over time, and the relationship between ENSO and hurricane data.

For the second phase, "Linear Regression Model," you create a model and note the observed and estimated hurricane occurrence over time, the relationship between the observed and estimated hurricane occurrence data with ENSO, and the residual hurricane occurrence values over time.

In the third phase, "Cross-validation," you explore the model's uncertainty. The basic idea is to simulate the exact forecast procedure for periods of the past and evaluate how well these simulated forecasts would have performed compared to the actual observations.

In the fourth and final phase, "Complete Forecast," you actually produce the forecast, using the model to generate a predictand value given a particular predictor value. Here, we also illustrate the uncertainty of the model predictions.

Please note that this exercise is a mechanism for explaining the concepts behind the development of a climate forecast. It should be understood as a theoretical exercise, not as a tool for application.

About Hurricanes in the Caribbean

According to the Hurricane Research Division of the US National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration "a tropical cyclone [or hurricane] is the generic term for a non-frontal synoptic scale low-pressure system over tropical or sub-tropical waters with organized convection (i.e. thunderstorm activity) and definite cyclonic surface wind circulation." Most hurricanes dissipate before making landfall. However, those that do reach populated areas have the potential to cause immense destruction, resulting in death, loss of livelihood, and the destruction of property.

More specifically, hurricanes can play a major role in impacting the water sector and water managers in the Caribbean. When Hurricane Dean passed over Barbados in 2007, for example, the storm disrupted a significant part of the island's water supply network, not through direct damage to supply and distribution infrastructure but indirectly through the impact of the electricity supply network. All pumping stations and booster pumps are connected to the electricity grid and therefore could not operate until power was restored.

Later on, as Hurricane Tomas hit St Lucia in 2010, precipitation caused by the storm triggered a series of landslides, including one into the John Compton Dam. These disrupted both the pumping station and the main transmission pipeline, such that water from the dam could not be extracted and the water supply was interrupted for several weeks. The landslide into the John Compton Dam also reduced the available storage volume as silt and debris emptied into the reservoir. Because of this reduced storage availability, St Lucia now experiences a very severe water supply crisis — the reservoir's water supply is critical and de-silting is thus complicated.

Impacts of Hurricane Tomas on the John Compton Dam in 2010 (Source)

The Hurricane Research Division identifies several key components to the development of hurricanes in the Atlantic Basin including:

  1. Sea surface temperature: Warm waters (at least 26.5ºC) provide the heat needed to power emerging storms.
  2. Moist mid-troposphere: This facilitates the development of thunderstorm activity.
  3. Pre-existing vorticity and convergence: This provides the context for hurricane development.
  4. Vertical wind shear: Vertical wind shear can be defined as "the magnitude of wind change with height." Intense vertical wind shear disrupts the emerging hurricane and can prevent its development. If a hurricane has already formed, strong vertical shear can diminish or eliminate the hurricane by interfering with the organization of deep convection around the cyclone center. Conversely, lessened vertical wind shear between the surface and the upper troposphere allows for the evolution of the storm.

Research has shown that El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) plays an important role in affecting vertical wind shear, as well as temperature at the Earth's surface and in the troposphere. This in turn is linked with hurricane activity throughout the Atlantic Basin.

As we discussed in Module, 2, research shows that the warm El Niño phase of the ENSO is associated with decreased hurricane activity in the Atlantic. Meanwhile, cooler La Niña events, which reduce vertical wind shear and increase vorticity, are associated with an increased probability of hurricane landfalls (Klotzbach, 2011). Tartaglione el al. (2003) specifically looked at the northern, eastern, and western regions of the Caribbean and determined that storms are significantly less frequent during El Niño years in the north, though there was not a significant change in storm frequency elsewhere throughout the region.

With the knowledge that ENSO plays a role in hurricane development in the Atlantic Basin, we can then begin to think about the possibility of forecasting hurricanes using ENSO information. That is, if we can predict an ENSO event, can we use this to predict the occurrence of a hurricane in the Caribbean? Let's continue on to the exercise to further explore this idea.
